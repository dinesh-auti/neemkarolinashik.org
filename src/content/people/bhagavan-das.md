Before he was *Bhagavan Das*, he was Kermit Michael Riggs, a Californian who left for India in the 1960s and spent years walking the subcontinent as a sadhu — six and a half feet tall, barefoot, hair to his waist, singing the names of God.

In 1967 he met a lost, searching Harvard professor named Richard Alpert and took him under his wing. It was Bhagavan Das who walked Alpert up into the Kumaon hills and into the presence of **Neem Karoli Baba** — the meeting that would turn Alpert into **Ram Dass**. The phrase that became the title of a generation's spiritual handbook, *Be Here Now*, came from Bhagavan Das's lips on those roads.

He later returned to the West, recorded kirtan, and continued to teach — but his place in this story is singular: he is the doorway through which Maharaj-ji's love first reached so many.
